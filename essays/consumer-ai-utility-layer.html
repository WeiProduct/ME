Consumer AI is not a chat box.

A thesis about where consumer AI wins, drawn from two years and seventeen shipped apps.

When I started WeiProduct in 2023, the obvious move was to build a chat wrapper. Pick a niche, build a chatbot for it, sell. Most people who started that way are gone.

Here is the pattern I see after shipping seventeen apps across five everyday-workflow categories: the consumer AI products that survive are the ones where the AI is invisible.

What chatbots get wrong

A chatbot asks the user to do the work twice. First they think of what they want. Then they translate that into a prompt. Then they evaluate the response. Then iterate. Each step is friction.

Real consumer habits do not want a translation step. When I open my calendar in the morning, I want to see my day — not have a conversation about it. When I track an expense, I want it logged in the smallest number of taps — not a chat about my spending patterns.

The AI’s job is not to be the interface. The AI’s job is to make the interface require fewer decisions.

Two layers, often confused

I think of consumer AI as having two layers, and they get conflated all the time.

The destination layer. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. You go there. The AI is the product. The conversation is the whole experience.

The utility layer. Every app you already use. The AI is hidden inside features that used to require thought.

Most consumer AI startups are still trying to compete in the destination layer. That fight is over. Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and Meta each already have a billion-dollar destination. There is no room for a seventeenth general-purpose chatbot.

The utility layer is where the next decade lives. It does not look impressive in demos. The product is the calendar that auto-fills, or the food logger that doesn’t need typing — not “the AI calendar you can chat with.”

Evidence from seventeen apps

You build a one-person studio by paying attention to which products keep people coming back. The ones that grow all sit in the utility layer.

Piggy Accounting does expense tracking. The AI is a quiet inference pass that categorizes each entry. The user never thinks about AI — they see “groceries · utilities · coffee.”

AI Voice Notes turns rambling speech into structured notes. The user speaks. Structure appears. No prompt.

AI Calendar auto-arranges by priority. The user adds events. The order corrects itself in the background.

The apps that stagnate are the ones I built closer to the destination model — the ones where the user has to engage with the AI before they get value. That extra step kills retention every time.

A test for builders

If you are building consumer AI in 2026, here is the test I run on every idea:

Could a sixty-year-old use this app and never realize there’s AI inside it?

If yes, you are building utility. The AI is doing real work the user never has to negotiate with. This is the surface where habits form.

If no — if the user has to talk to it, prompt it, interpret its output — you are competing in the destination layer. Against trillion-dollar incumbents. With less data, fewer users, and no distribution advantage. There is room for one chatbot per category. Not seventeen.

So pick a habit. Build the smallest possible product that removes one decision from that habit. Hide the AI inside it. Ship in a week, not a month. Iterate based on retention, not engagement.

What I am working on next

The thesis is also a constraint on what I build. The studio has seventeen apps because the cost of testing the thesis is low — you ship narrow, you watch retention, you compound the ones that work. An in-house toolkit (SwiftUI components, auth, IAP, telemetry) cuts new-app time-to-store by roughly sixty percent. The marginal cost of being wrong about an idea is small. The marginal cost of being slow to test is large.

The next seventeen will be sharper. The ones still in the destination model are getting deprecated. The ones in the utility layer are getting better.
